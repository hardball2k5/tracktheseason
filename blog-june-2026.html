Monthly Pace Report July 1, 2026

MLB June 2026 Pace Report: The Season Is Starting to Take Shape

June separated contenders from pretenders. Traditional standings will catch up eventually — but pace data already knows who's pulling away and who's fading fast.

By Track The Season · ~12 min read · Data through June 30, 2026

By the time April ends, every team is still mathematically alive and fan optimism is a renewable resource. May narrows the field. But June? June is where baseball's actual story gets written. The pace data that Track The Season tracks — comparing every team to their own history at the exact same point in the season — has spent the last thirty days drawing a cleaner picture than anything you'll find in the traditional standings.

Here's what's real, what's noise, and what July is going to be decided by.

Number of the Month
14
MLB teams are running 5 or more wins ahead or behind last year's pace through the same games played. The Chaos Index is elevated — and the standings haven't caught up yet.

Why Pace Matters More Than the Standings Right Now

Here's the thing about standings: they're a snapshot of everything that's already happened. They reward hot starts and punish slow ones equally, regardless of what the team is doing right now. A team that went 25–10 in April and 9–18 in June looks fine in the standings. They're not fine.

Track The Season's pace metrics cut through this by comparing every team's current win total to their win total at the exact same games-played point in prior seasons. A team currently at 47–38 through 85 games isn't interesting as a raw number. But if they were 38–47 at this point last year, that's a 9-win improvement in the same amount of time — and that's a story worth paying attention to.

Story of the Month: Milwaukee Has Become a Legitimate Contender

At the start of June, the Brewers were a good team. By the end of it, they had become something harder to dismiss. Milwaukee's climb to a 100+ win pace is the most significant development of the month — not just because of the number, but because of how they got there.

The exclamation point was Jacob Misiorowski's complete-game shutout against Philadelphia on June 28th. Complete games are rare enough in 2026 that they register as events, not just box scores. Against a Phillies lineup that ranks among the most dangerous in the National League, Misiorowski didn't just win — he dominated, inducing weak contact early and finishing with conviction. It was the kind of performance that changes how a pitching staff sees itself for the rest of the season.

But one game doesn't build a 100-win pace. What the Brewers have done in June is sustained the kind of pitching-first, opportunistic offense identity that historically produces October runs. Their rotation depth — something the Pitching Board has tracked all season — has become genuinely elite. Patrick and Harrison as a 1-2 punch project to a combined win pace that ranks among the best in the National League.

The Dodgers Still Set the Standard — But It's Worth Watching Their Pace

Los Angeles has baseball's best record. Dave Roberts' milestone win earlier this month underscored just how consistently this franchise has been run. The Dodgers are not in danger of collapsing. But pace data tells a nuanced story worth paying attention to heading into July.

The Dodgers' current season pace sits at 105W — elite by any measure. What's interesting is the L20 trend. Their last 20 games have run closer to a 95W pace, suggesting a modest cooling from their dominant April and May. In isolation, 95W over 20 games is still excellent. In context of what the Dodgers have historically expected from themselves at this point — and what their payroll demands — it's a minor flag worth monitoring.

None of this should concern Dodgers fans meaningfully. History shows that elite Los Angeles teams run at slightly lower paces in June as the rotation naturally cycles through less dominant stretches. What matters is July trajectory. If the Dodgers' L20 pace climbs back toward 105W, the World Series conversation becomes inevitable. If it holds at 95W, the NL West will be more interesting than the standings suggest.

Best Historical Comparison
The 2026 Dodgers pace most closely tracks the 2023 Dodgers at the same 85-game mark. That team finished with 100 wins and made a deep playoff run. The ceiling here is well-established — the question is consistency through the second half.

The Phillies Are Climbing Again — and Their L20 Says It's Real

Philadelphia entered June with a troubling gap: their full-season pace was fine, but their recent form was quietly slipping. That trend has reversed. The Phillies' last 20 games have moved their momentum delta from negative territory into positive ground, and that's not a small thing for a team with World Series expectations.

The key is what the pace data shows beneath the surface. Philadelphia started the season 18–22 through 40 games — a slow-burn start by their own historical standards. Their pace since then has been dramatically better. That's the kind of split the Phillies' team page reveals clearly: a team that got their identity back and is now building genuine momentum heading into the stretch run.

Misiorowski's complete game against them notwithstanding, Philadelphia looks more like a playoff threat today than they did on June 1st. Their recent L10 pace ranks among the best in the NL East. Whether that momentum survives the July schedule gauntlet will define whether 2026 is a wild card year or a division race.

The Yankees Need to Reverse Momentum Before It Costs Them

New York's season pace is still respectable — they remain a playoff-caliber team on paper. But pace data through June tells a cautionary tale that the wins-and-losses column hasn't fully reflected yet. The Yankees' recent form has been materially worse than their season average, and that gap has widened during the month.

The pattern is recognizable. A strong start inflates a team's season pace. The team then plays at a lower rate for an extended stretch. The standings still look fine because the bank of early wins provides cover. But the pace trend line is pointing the wrong direction, and historically, teams that enter July with a significant negative momentum delta — where recent form is running well below season average — tend to finish closer to their recent pace than their early-season one.

For the Yankees, the question entering July isn't whether they make the playoffs — their season pace still projects them comfortably in. The question is whether they're built for October or just another Wild Card team that goes out in the first round. The L20 data suggests the latter if they don't find a way to reverse course.

The Marlins Are the Biggest Surprise in Baseball

Miami deserves its own paragraph. The Marlins' pace advantage versus last season is the largest positive delta in the sport — a number so large it initially looked like a data error. It isn't. Miami has built something quietly legitimate, and the fact that national coverage has been slow to catch up makes it a better story, not a lesser one. Track their season on Track The Season and you'll see what the standings don't show: a team playing with genuine momentum and a rotation that is outperforming even modest expectations.

Teams Trending the Wrong Way

Not every June story is a rising one. Three teams in particular saw their pace outlook deteriorate in ways the raw standings don't yet fully capture.

The Mets: Still Talented, But the Pace Gap Is Growing

New York's Mets remain a roster with legitimate quality. But their recent pace has dropped significantly below their season average, and that gap has widened throughout June. The Mets' pace page tells the story cleanly: a team that built its record on a strong start is now playing closer to the fringe-of-contention level it may actually be. Panic is too strong a word. But July will clarify whether this is a slump or a reveal.

The Red Sox: The Gap Between Expectation and Reality

Boston fans know the feeling. The Red Sox entered June with a pace that suggested they might be rounding into form. They leave it with a negative momentum delta and questions about their rotation depth that the Pitching Board has been flagging for weeks. The gap between what Boston's payroll suggests they should be and what their current pace reflects is one of the most significant payroll efficiency stories of the first half.

The Padres: A Summer to Figure Out

San Diego's pace decline in June was the most concerning of the three because it didn't come with an obvious injury explanation. The Padres are simply not playing at the level their season started to suggest, and their L20 pace puts them closer to a .500 team than the competitor their roster was constructed to be. July is a make-or-break month for whether 2026 becomes a serious run or another missed opportunity.

Biggest Surprise: Miami Has No Business Being This Good

At the start of the season, almost no projection system had the Marlins in a serious playoff conversation. Their payroll was among the lowest in baseball. Their roster had talent but no clear identity. And yet here we are, July 1st, with Miami sitting on one of the largest positive year-over-year pace improvements in the sport.

The surprise isn't just that they're winning. It's the manner of it. Their rotation has been efficient. Their offense has been opportunistic rather than powerful. And their manager has gotten the most out of a roster that most analysts wrote off before Opening Day. This is exactly the kind of team the Chaos Report was built to capture — a team running a genuinely different season than expected, and doing it with enough consistency that it's no longer a hot streak. It's a trend.

Biggest Disappointment: San Diego's Squandered Window

The Padres built a roster to compete in 2026. Their payroll reflects it. Their June pace does not. This is the most disappointing story of the month precisely because the talent is undeniably there. The Padres have the pieces for a deep run. What they lack right now is the consistency that separates good teams from great ones, and their pace decline in June is the clearest evidence that something isn't clicking at the organizational level.

Team Under the Radar: The Twins

While the Brewers earned most of the AL Central attention in June, the Twins have been quietly building a compelling pace story of their own. Paredes and Abel as a 1-2 combination have been producing at an elite level in their starts, and Minnesota's overall pace projects them into a legitimate wild card position that the national conversation hasn't fully acknowledged yet. Worth watching in July.

Looking Ahead to July: The Questions That Matter

July is when the season's actual narrative gets locked in. Trade deadlines, schedule difficulty, and the cumulative weight of 90-plus games separate teams with sustainable winning patterns from those built on variance. Here are the five questions Track The Season's pace data thinks July will answer.

Can Milwaukee sustain a 100-win pace through a tougher second-half schedule?

The Brewers' rotation depth gives them the foundation. What they'll be tested on is whether that depth holds as the opposition quality increases. Misiorowski's shutout suggested it can. Forty more starts will confirm it.

Can Philadelphia keep climbing, or is this another false summit?

The Phillies have pulled this move before — build momentum, look dangerous, then plateau. Their L20 pace heading into July is the best indicator that this time might be different. Compare them to the Braves and you'll see two teams moving in opposite directions.

Will the Yankees reverse course, or is the decline structural?

New York's roster is still too good to dismiss. But the pace trend has been negative for long enough that it's no longer noise. July will either reveal a team that found its form or one that needed a trade deadline move that may or may not happen.

Can Miami keep surprising everyone?

The biggest test for the Marlins is whether their pace holds as the league adjusts to what they're doing. Pace sustainability is Track The Season's core metric — and right now, Miami's is as positive as anyone's in baseball.

Which rivalry becomes baseball's biggest July story?

The data says Phillies vs Mets. The pace trajectories of those two teams are moving in opposite directions simultaneously, which historically produces the kind of division swing that makes September irrelevant. If Philadelphia's momentum continues and New York's decline deepens, the NL East race is over before August.

Most Interesting Pace Trend of June
The Braves' L20 pace collapse. Atlanta entered June as a 96W-pace team. Their last 20 games project to a 57W pace. That's a -39W momentum delta — the largest negative gap of any contending team in the sport. Either this is a correctable slump, or the Braves' early-season hot start has masked a team that's actually much closer to average. July will answer that question definitively. Frequently Asked Questions

Which MLB teams are heating up in June 2026?

The Milwaukee Brewers, Miami Marlins, and Philadelphia Phillies showed the strongest positive momentum in June 2026 based on Track The Season's pace analysis. The Brewers reached a 100+ win pace, while Miami posted the largest positive year-over-year delta in the sport.

Which MLB teams are cooling off in June 2026?

The New York Mets, Boston Red Sox, and San Diego Padres showed the most significant pace declines in June 2026. The Braves had the largest single-month momentum delta decline, dropping from a 96W season pace to a 57W pace over their last 20 games.

What is MLB win pace and how does Track The Season calculate it?

Win pace is a team's current win percentage projected over a full 162-game season. Track The Season also compares pace to prior seasons at the same games-played point, revealing whether a team is ahead of, behind, or matching its own historical performance — something traditional standings cannot show.
